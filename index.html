How zone classification works

FloodPath maps a weighted sum of inputs onto one of three tiers. High risk (≥60) triggers immediate action alerts. Medium (35–59) surfaces drainage recommendations. Low (<35) confirms baseline safety.

Thresholds are calibrated against historical flood event data — each boundary represents a meaningful jump in expected damage probability.

The scoring breakdown

Each factor contributes a weighted sub-score. Elevation and proximity carry the most weight since they're the strongest physical predictors of flood exposure.

Drainage and region-specific factors fine-tune the score, letting FloodPath distinguish two zones with identical geography but different infrastructure.

Urban vs rural risk logic

Urban zones use impervious surface coverage — the share of concrete and asphalt that blocks water absorption. High coverage = more runoff = greater flood exposure.

Rural zones use soil permeability — how quickly the ground absorbs water. Low permeability (clay soils) means runoff builds just like urban concrete.
